1Surprise: how shocked should you be?h(x) = −log p(x)

Information theory starts with one question: when an event happens, how much did you learn? A sunrise tells you nothing — you knew it was coming. A coin landing on its edge tells you a lot. So "information" should be large when probability is small. Bishop writes this as the surprise (information content) $h(x) = -\log_2 p(x)$, measured in bits.

Slide the probability of an event and watch its surprise. At $p=\tfrac12$ a coin flip carries exactly 1 bit. A 1-in-1024 event carries 10 bits. As $p\to0$, surprise $\to\infty$.

2Entropy: surprise you expect before lookingH[x] = −Σ p log p

Surprise is per-outcome. Entropy is the average surprise of the whole distribution — how uncertain you are before the draw. It's the expectation of $h(x)$: $H[x] = -\sum_x p(x)\log_2 p(x)$. Two anchors: a sure thing has $H=0$ (no surprise possible), and the uniform distribution maximizes entropy ($H=\log_2 M$ for $M$ outcomes).

2a · The two-outcome case (a biased coin)

For a coin with $P(\text{heads})=p$, entropy traces the famous binary entropy curve. It peaks at $p=\tfrac12$ (a fair coin: maximum uncertainty, 1 bit) and falls to 0 at both ends (a rigged coin: no uncertainty).

3Entropy is a coding budgetShannon, 1948

Entropy isn't abstract — it's the fewest bits per symbol any code can achieve. Shannon's noiseless coding theorem says the optimal codeword length for a symbol of probability $p$ is $-\log_2 p$ bits: frequent symbols get short codes, rare ones get long codes. The average code length then equals the entropy. (This is exactly the intuition behind BPE tokenization and why perplexity matters.)

Drag the bars to change symbol frequencies. Each block's width is its probability $p_k$ and its height is its ideal code length $-\log_2 p_k$. So each block's area is $p_k\cdot(-\log_2 p_k)$ — and the total shaded area is the entropy: the average number of bits to transmit one symbol. Common symbols are wide and short; rare ones are thin and tall.

4Where the formula comes from: counting arrangements

Bishop's §2.5.2 derives entropy from physics. Throw $N$ objects into bins. A macrostate is just the histogram of counts $\{n_i\}$; its multiplicity $W = N!/\prod_i n_i!$ counts how many distinct arrangements produce it. Take logs, apply Stirling, and out pops $\frac1N\ln W \to -\sum_i p_i \ln p_i = H$. The uniform histogram has overwhelmingly the most arrangements — which is why disorder (and entropy) is what you generically see.

5Continuous entropy can go negativedifferential entropy

For continuous variables, $H[x] = -\int p(x)\ln p(x)\,dx$. Two surprises: it can be negative, and it's not invariant to how you parameterize $x$. Both come from the same place — a density isn't a probability. For a Gaussian, $H = \tfrac12\ln(2\pi e\sigma^2)$ nats, which dips below zero once $\sigma$ gets small enough that the peak exceeds 1.

6The Gaussian is the most honest guessmax entropy

§2.5.4: among all distributions with a given mean and variance, the Gaussian has the highest differential entropy. "Highest entropy" = "fewest hidden assumptions." If all you know is a mean and a spread, any non-Gaussian choice smuggles in extra structure you can't justify. This is the principled reason Gaussian noise shows up in diffusion models and Gaussian inits in your networks.

All four densities have identical mean (0) and variance (1) — same "size." Yet their entropies differ, and the Gaussian tops the bar chart. The uniform and triangular are "too confined"; the Laplace is "too pinched." The Gaussian threads the needle: the least-committal shape consistent with the constraints.

7KL divergence: the cost of believing the wrong modelCRITICAL

$\text{KL}(p\,\|\,q) = \int p(x)\ln\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}\,dx$ measures how far an approximation $q$ is from the truth $p$. Operationally: if reality is $p$ but you build your code/model assuming $q$, KL is the extra bits you waste per sample. It's always $\ge 0$, zero only when $q=p$ — but it is not symmetric.

Why minimizing KL = maximum likelihood
Cross-entropy splits exactly: $\;H(p,q) = \underbrace{H(p)}_{\text{irreducible}} + \underbrace{\text{KL}(p\,\|\,q)}_{\text{your error}}$. The first term is fixed by reality. So minimizing cross-entropy loss over your model $q_\theta$ is precisely minimizing $\text{KL}(p_{\text{data}}\|q_\theta)$ — which is maximum likelihood. This single line is why LLM training works.
The stacked bar tracks the figure above. The blue floor $H(p)$ never moves — it's the true entropy of the data, the best any model could do. The red stack on top is your KL error. Training pushes the red to zero; it can never dig below the blue floor.

8Forward vs reverse KL: cover everything, or commit?RLHF · VI · distillation

KL's asymmetry is not a footnote — it decides the behavior of half of modern ML. Fit a single Gaussian $q$ to a stubbornly bimodal truth $p$ and the two directions disagree completely:

  • Forward, $\text{KL}(p\,\|\,q)$ — "mean-seeking / mode-covering". $p$ multiplies the integrand, so $q$ is punished wherever $p$ has mass but $q$ doesn't. To avoid infinite penalty $q$ stretches to blanket both modes, even piling mass in the empty valley between them. This is the MLE / distillation direction.
  • Reverse, $\text{KL}(q\,\|\,p)$ — "mode-seeking". Now $q$ multiplies the integrand and is punished for putting mass where $p$ is near zero. The safe move is to collapse onto a single mode and ignore the rest. This is the variational-inference direction, and the spirit of RLHF's KL leash that keeps a policy inside the reference model's support.

Lesson 1 / 2 · the objective

The KL-regularized objective & its closed-form optimum

Maximizing reward outright lets the policy chase reward-model artifacts and forget how to speak. RLHF instead maximizes reward while staying close to the frozen reference via a reverse-KL leash — exactly the mode-seeking direction from the lesson above:

$$\max_{\pi}\; \mathbb{E}_{y\sim\pi}\big[\textcolor{#f6c945}{r(y)}\big]\;-\;\textcolor{#c08cf0}{\beta}\,\mathrm{KL}\!\big(\textcolor{#54d6bd}{\pi}\,\Vert\,\textcolor{#5ec8e8}{\pi_{\text{ref}}}\big)$$

Setting the functional derivative to zero and normalizing gives a closed-form optimum:

$$\textcolor{#54d6bd}{\pi^\star(y)}=\frac{1}{Z}\,\textcolor{#5ec8e8}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y)}\,\exp\!\Big(\tfrac{\textcolor{#f6c945}{r(y)}}{\textcolor{#c08cf0}{\beta}}\Big),\qquad Z=\sum_y \textcolor{#5ec8e8}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y)}\,e^{r(y)/\beta}$$
  1. A reward model scores each candidate response: $r(y)$ (gold numbers on the chart).
  2. The leash multiplies the reference by an exponential tilt $e^{r/\beta}$ — good responses boosted, bad ones suppressed.
  3. $\beta$ sets how hard you pull: large $\beta$ → hug $\pi_{\text{ref}}$; small $\beta$ → collapse onto the single highest-reward response (reward-hacking territory).

Lesson 2 / 2 · the optimizer

How PPO actually climbs toward $\pi^\star$

We never have $\pi^\star$ in closed form — $Z$ sums over all sequences. PPO approaches it from sampled rollouts with two pieces.

(a) Per-token reward shaping. The KL leash is folded into the reward at every token:

$$\tilde r_t = \underbrace{r_{\text{RM}}\,[t{=}T]}_{\text{terminal RM score}} \;-\; \textcolor{#c08cf0}{\beta}\,\big(\log\textcolor{#54d6bd}{\pi_\theta(a_t|s_t)}-\log\textcolor{#5ec8e8}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(a_t|s_t)}\big)$$

(b) The clipped surrogate. With ratio $\textcolor{#c08cf0}{\rho_t}=\dfrac{\pi_\theta(a_t|s_t)}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(a_t|s_t)}$ and advantage $\hat A_t$ (from GAE on $\tilde r$):

$$L^{\text{CLIP}}=\mathbb{E}\Big[\min\big(\textcolor{#c08cf0}{\rho_t}\,\hat A_t,\;\operatorname{clip}(\textcolor{#c08cf0}{\rho_t},1-\epsilon,1+\epsilon)\,\hat A_t\big)\Big]$$
  1. If advantage $\hat A>0$ (better than expected), raising $\rho$ helps — but the clip at $1+\epsilon$ caps the gain so one step can't move the policy too far.
  2. If advantage $\hat A<0$ (worse), lowering $\rho$ helps — clipped at $1-\epsilon$.
  3. The flat clipped regions have zero gradient → the trust region that keeps updates honest. That's PPO's whole trick.
The solid curve is the clipped surrogate vs the ratio ρ (green for $\hat A>0$, red for $\hat A<0$); the grey dashed line is the unclipped $\rho\hat A$. Outside $[1-\epsilon,1+\epsilon]$ (gold lines) the surrogate goes flat — no incentive to keep moving.
Lesson 1 / 2 · the reparameterization

Turning the RL optimum into a reward

DPO's insight: the RLHF optimum above can be algebraically inverted. Take logs of $\pi^\star$ and solve for the reward:

$$\textcolor{#f6c945}{r(y)} = \textcolor{#c08cf0}{\beta}\,\log\frac{\textcolor{#54d6bd}{\pi^\star(y)}}{\textcolor{#5ec8e8}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y)}} \;+\; \textcolor{#8a8a9c}{\beta\log Z}$$

So any policy implicitly defines a reward: its log-ratio to the reference, scaled by β, up to the per-prompt constant $\beta\log Z$. The chart shows the true reward (gold) and the recovered reward $\hat r(y)=\beta\log(\pi^\star/\pi_{\text{ref}})$ — identical shape, shifted down by exactly $\beta\log Z$.

Lesson 2 / 2 · the cancellation

Bradley–Terry + the vanishing $Z$ → the DPO loss

Preferences use Bradley–Terry: the chance the winner $y_w$ beats the loser $y_l$ is a sigmoid of their reward gap, $P(y_w\succ y_l)=\sigma(r_w-r_l)$. Substitute the implicit reward — and the ugly $\beta\log Z$ cancels, because it's identical for both responses to the same prompt:

$$r_w-r_l=\textcolor{#c08cf0}{\beta}\Big(\underbrace{\log\tfrac{\pi_\theta(y_w)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_w)}}_{\textcolor{#84d98a}{s_w}}-\underbrace{\log\tfrac{\pi_\theta(y_l)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_l)}}_{\textcolor{#fc6a5d}{s_l}}\Big)+\textcolor{#8a8a9c}{\cancel{\beta\log Z}}$$

leaving a pure classification loss — no reward model, no RL loop:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{DPO}}=-\log\sigma\!\Big(\textcolor{#c08cf0}{\beta}\big(\textcolor{#84d98a}{s_w}-\textcolor{#fc6a5d}{s_l}\big)\Big)$$
  1. Compute each response's log-ratio against the reference: $s_w$, $s_l$.
  2. Form the margin $m=\beta(s_w-s_l)$ — how much more the model prefers the winner than the reference did.
  3. Loss $=-\log\sigma(m)$. Its gradient is weighted by $\sigma(-m)$ = "how wrong we still are" — confident-correct pairs barely contribute.

Lesson 1 / 2 · soft targets

Temperature & "dark knowledge"

A trained teacher's top class is usually obvious; the value is in the relative probabilities of the runner-ups ("a 7 looks a bit like a 1, never like a 9"). A cold softmax buries this. Distillation heats up the softmax with temperature $T$:

$$\textcolor{#5ec8e8}{p^{\,T}_i}=\frac{\exp(z_i/\textcolor{#c08cf0}{T})}{\sum_j\exp(z_j/\textcolor{#c08cf0}{T})}$$
  1. At $T=1$ the distribution is sharp — one tall bar.
  2. Raising $T$ flattens it, lifting the small "dark-knowledge" bars into view.
  3. As $T\to\infty$ it approaches uniform (all structure gone). The useful range is intermediate.

Lesson 2 / 2 · the objective

Distillation is a forward KL

The student $q_S$ is trained to match the softened teacher $p^T$ by minimizing the forward KL — the mean-seeking direction from the lesson above, so the student is pressured to put mass on every class the teacher likes:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{KD}}=\textcolor{#c08cf0}{T^2}\cdot\mathrm{KL}\!\big(\textcolor{#5ec8e8}{p^{\,T}}\,\Vert\,\textcolor{#54d6bd}{q^{\,T}_S}\big)$$
  1. Soften both teacher and student logits by the same $T$.
  2. Minimize forward KL → student covers all teacher modes (reverse KL would let it collapse onto the top class).
  3. The $T^2$ prefactor undoes the $1/T^2$ shrinkage that softening puts on the gradients, keeping this term comparable to the hard-label loss it's mixed with.

Lesson 1 / 2 · the ELBO

ELBO = reconstruction − KL-to-prior

A VAE can't compute $\log p(x)$, so it maximizes a lower bound (the ELBO):

$$\log p(x)\;\ge\;\underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{\textcolor{#54d6bd}{q(z|x)}}[\log p(x|z)]}_{\text{reconstruction}}\;-\;\underbrace{\mathrm{KL}\!\big(\textcolor{#54d6bd}{q(z|x)}\,\Vert\,\textcolor{#5ec8e8}{p(z)}\big)}_{\text{regularizer}}$$

With encoder $q(z|x)=\mathcal N(\mu,\sigma^2)$ and prior $p(z)=\mathcal N(0,1)$, the KL has a closed form you can read term by term:

$$\mathrm{KL}=\tfrac12\big(\;\underbrace{\textcolor{#c08cf0}{\mu^2}}_{\text{pulls }\mu\to0}\;+\;\underbrace{\textcolor{#f49d52}{\sigma^2-1-\log\sigma^2}}_{\text{pulls }\sigma\to1}\;\big)$$

Lesson 2 / 2 · the trick & the trap

Reparameterization & posterior collapse

You can't backprop through "sample $z\sim q$". The reparameterization trick rewrites the sample as a deterministic, differentiable function of fixed noise:

$$z=\textcolor{#c08cf0}{\mu}+\textcolor{#5ec8e8}{\sigma}\odot\epsilon,\qquad \epsilon\sim\mathcal N(0,1)$$

Gradients now flow through μ and σ; the randomness sits in $\epsilon$, off the gradient path. β-VAE weights the regularizer by β — and there's a trap: push β too hard and the encoder finds it cheapest to just become the prior (posterior collapse), so the latent carries no information.

  1. For a toy datapoint that "wants" the code $\mu^\star{=}2$, balancing reconstruction against βKL gives $\textcolor{#54d6bd}{\mu^\star}=\frac{2}{1+\beta}$, $\textcolor{#5ec8e8}{\sigma^{\star2}}=\frac{\beta}{1+\beta}$.
  2. Small β → $\mu^\star\to2,\ \sigma^\star\to0$: a crisp, informative code.
  3. Large β → $\mu^\star\to0,\ \sigma^\star\to1$: the posterior collapses onto the prior and the decoder ignores $z$.

10Mutual information: how much one variable tells you about another

Conditional entropy $H[Y|X]$ is the uncertainty left in $Y$ after you see $X$, and the chain rule stacks cleanly: $H[X,Y] = H[X] + H[Y|X]$. Mutual information is what observing $X$ removes from $Y$ (or vice-versa): $I[X,Y] = H[Y] - H[Y|X] = \text{KL}\big(p(x,y)\,\|\,p(x)p(y)\big)$ — literally the KL distance from "$X,Y$ independent." It's symmetric, $\ge 0$, and zero iff independent.

The continuous cousin: correlated Gaussians

For a bivariate Gaussian, mutual information has a clean closed form, $I = -\tfrac12\log_2(1-\rho^2)$ bits. At $\rho=0$ the cloud is round and $X$ says nothing about $Y$. As $|\rho|\to1$ the cloud collapses to a line and $I\to\infty$ — knowing $X$ pins down $Y$. This is the quantity InfoNCE / CLIP push up between paired views, and the information bottleneck pushes down between a representation and its input.

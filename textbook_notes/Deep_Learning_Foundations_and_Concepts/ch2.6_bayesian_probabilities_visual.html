of a weight

§2.6 reframes "training" as inference: a parameter isn't a number to find, it's a belief to update. This page builds that intuition for 2.6.1 (model parameters) and 2.6.2 (regularization as MAP), then cashes it out in the LLM tricks it secretly explains — ensembles, model soups / SWA, weight decay, AdamW, and LoRA. Everything is live; drag and slide.

2.6.1From a number to a belief: posterior ∝ likelihood × prior

Bayes treats the parameters $\mathbf w$ as a random variable. You start with a prior belief, the data reweights it through the likelihood, and the product (renormalized) is the posterior:

$$\underbrace{\textcolor{#54d6bd}{p(\mathbf w|\mathcal D)}}_{\text{posterior}}=\frac{\overbrace{\textcolor{#f6c945}{p(\mathcal D|\mathbf w)}}^{\text{likelihood}}\;\overbrace{\textcolor{#5ec8e8}{p(\mathbf w)}}^{\text{prior}}}{\underbrace{p(\mathcal D)}_{\text{evidence}}}\;\;\propto\;\;\textcolor{#f6c945}{p(\mathcal D|\mathbf w)}\,\textcolor{#5ec8e8}{p(\mathbf w)}$$

Toy problem: infer the unknown mean $\mu$ of data with known noise $\sigma^2{=}1$. Prior $\textcolor{#5ec8e8}{\mu\sim\mathcal N(0,s^2)}$. With $N$ points of sample-mean $\bar x$, the posterior is Gaussian with mean $\textcolor{#54d6bd}{\mu_N}=\bar x\,\frac{N}{N+1/s^2}$ — a tug-of-war between 0 and the data.

Add data (slide $N$ up) and the posterior marches from the prior toward the data and gets narrower — certainty grows. Tighten the prior (small $s$) and it resists the data longer. This is learning: the prior is your init/regularizer, the likelihood is your loss, the posterior is what you should believe.

2.6.1Why a point estimate throws away information

Standard training returns one winner $\mathbf w_{\text{ML}}$. The Bayesian predictive instead integrates over every plausible $\mathbf w$, weighted by the posterior:

$$p(t|x,\mathcal D)=\int \underbrace{p(t|x,\textcolor{#54d6bd}{\mathbf w})}_{\text{one model}}\;\underbrace{\textcolor{#54d6bd}{p(\mathbf w|\mathcal D)}}_{\text{its weight}}\,d\mathbf w$$

The payoff is honest uncertainty: where data is dense, all plausible models agree (tight band); where data is absent, they fan out (wide band). A single point estimate is blind to this — it draws one confident line through regions it knows nothing about.

2.6.1You can't integrate a billion parameters — so you sample themLLM

That integral over $\mathbf w$ is hopeless at LLM scale. Every practical "uncertainty" trick is a cheap discrete stand-in for it:

  • Deep ensembles — train $K$ models, average predictions ≈ a $K$-sample Monte-Carlo estimate of the posterior integral.
  • Model soups / SWA — average the weights of several checkpoints into one model. Far cheaper (one model at inference) but only valid when the checkpoints sit in a connected low-loss basin.
  • LoRA ensembles — average a handful of cheap adapters for the same effect during fine-tuning.

The catch lives in weight space. Averaging predictions is always safe; averaging weights is only safe along a low-loss path between the points (linear mode connectivity).

2.6.2Regularization is just a prior in disguise

Take $-\ln$ of the posterior and the product becomes a sum — your loss plus a penalty that is literally the negative log-prior:

$$-\ln \textcolor{#54d6bd}{p(\mathbf w|\mathcal D)}=\underbrace{-\ln \textcolor{#f6c945}{p(\mathcal D|\mathbf w)}}_{\text{data loss}}\;\underbrace{-\ln \textcolor{#c08cf0}{p(\mathbf w)}}_{\text{regularizer}}+\,\text{const}$$

A Gaussian prior $\mathcal N(0,s^2)$ gives the penalty $\textcolor{#c08cf0}{\tfrac{1}{2s^2}\sum_i w_i^2}$ — exactly $L_2$ / weight decay, with $\lambda=\tfrac{1}{2s^2}$. A Laplace prior gives $\textcolor{#c08cf0}{\lambda\sum_i|w_i|}$ — $L_1$, which prefers exact zeros (sparsity). MAP is the peak of the posterior: where loss + penalty is smallest.

The 2-D picture of why L1 zeroes weights

With two weights, the MAP estimate is where the loss contours first touch the prior's "budget" region. The $L_2$ ball is round, so contact is generically off-axis — both weights nonzero, just smaller. The $L_1$ diamond has corners on the axes, and contours tend to hit a corner — so one weight snaps to exactly zero. That geometry is the whole story of sparsity.

2.6.2Why AdamW ≠ Adam + L2LLM

If weight decay is a Gaussian prior, you'd want it applied uniformly — every weight believed equally likely to be near zero. Adam breaks that. Its update divides the gradient by a per-parameter scale $\sqrt{\hat v}$ (the running gradient magnitude):

$$\textbf{Adam+L2:}\quad \mathbf w \leftarrow \mathbf w-\eta\,\frac{\hat m + \textcolor{#fc6a5d}{\lambda\mathbf w}}{\sqrt{\hat v}+\epsilon}\qquad\Rightarrow\qquad \text{effective decay}=\frac{\lambda}{\sqrt{\hat v}}$$
$$\textbf{AdamW:}\quad \mathbf w \leftarrow \mathbf w-\eta\,\frac{\hat m}{\sqrt{\hat v}+\epsilon}-\eta\,\textcolor{#54d6bd}{\lambda\mathbf w}\qquad\Rightarrow\qquad \text{effective decay}=\lambda$$

Folding $\lambda\mathbf w$ inside the $\sqrt{\hat v}$ division means high-gradient weights get decayed less — the prior strength leaks out of your control. AdamW decouples the decay so it lands uniformly, as a Gaussian prior should.

Toy: a 2-D weight whose data optimum is w★=(2.5, 2.5) but whose loss is steep in $w_1$, flat in $w_2$ (so $\hat v_1\gg\hat v_2$). Decay should pull both toward the origin equally.

2.6.2LoRA is a low-rank prior on the updateLLM

Weight decay is a weak, isotropic prior ("stay smallish"). LoRA imposes a far stronger structural prior on the fine-tuning update: force it to be low rank,

$$\Delta W=\textcolor{#54d6bd}{B}\,\textcolor{#5ec8e8}{A},\qquad \textcolor{#54d6bd}{B}\in\mathbb R^{d\times r},\;\textcolor{#5ec8e8}{A}\in\mathbb R^{r\times d},\quad r\ll d$$

Instead of $d^2$ free numbers, you get $2dr$. The bet (Aghajanyan et al. 2021): fine-tuning updates already live in a tiny subspace, so a rank-$r$ slice loses almost nothing. The thread tying §2.6 together
A weight is a belief. Training sharpens the prior into a posterior (2.6.1). Regularization is the prior's fingerprint on the loss (2.6.2). Everything else — ensembles and soups (sampling the posterior), AdamW (applying the prior faithfully), LoRA (a strong structural prior) — is engineering around the one integral we can't actually compute.
